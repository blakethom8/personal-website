## 🔑 Landmark Studies

### 1. Healthcare Waste Taxonomy — Shrank et al. (2019) ⭐

**Citation**: Shrank WH, Rogstad TL, Parekh N. "Waste in the US Health Care System: Estimated Costs and Potential for Savings." *JAMA*. 2019;322(15):1501-1509.  
**DOI**: [10.1001/jama.2019.13978](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2019.13978)  
**PMID**: 31589283

**Key findings**:
- Total annual cost of waste: **$760 billion to $935 billion** (~25% of total healthcare spending)
- Six domains of waste (with ranges):
  1. **Failure of care delivery**: $102.4B - $165.7B
  2. **Failure of care coordination**: $27.2B - $78.2B
  3. **Overtreatment/low-value care**: $75.7B - $101.2B
  4. **Pricing failure**: $230.7B - $240.5B
  5. **Fraud and abuse**: $58.5B - $83.9B
  6. **Administrative complexity**: $265.6B (single estimate)
- Potential savings from interventions: $191B - $286B (~25% reduction in waste)
- **No studies identified targeting administrative complexity interventions**
- Based on synthesis of 71 estimates from 54 publications (2012-2019)

**Relevance**: This is THE definitive waste taxonomy. Note that administrative complexity is the largest single category, and pricing failure is second. Together they account for ~$500B+ of the waste.

### 2. US vs. Canada Administrative Costs — Himmelstein et al. (2020) ⭐

**Citation**: Himmelstein DU, Campbell T, Woolhandler S. "Health Care Administrative Costs in the United States and Canada, 2017." *Annals of Internal Medicine*. 2020;172(2):134-142.  
**DOI**: [10.7326/M19-2818](https://doi.org/10.7326/M19-2818)  
**PMID**: 31905376

**Key findings**:
- US administrative spending in 2017: **$812 billion** ($2,497 per capita, **34.2% of NHE**)
- Canada administrative spending: $551 per capita (**17.0% of NHE**)
- Breakdown of US $2,497 per capita:
  - **Insurers' overhead**: $844 (vs. $146 in Canada)
  - **Hospital administration**: $933 (vs. $196 in Canada)
  - **Nursing home/home care/hospice admin**: $255 (vs. $123 in Canada)
  - **Physicians' insurance-related costs**: $465 (vs. $87 in Canada)
- Since 1999, admin share grew 3.2 percentage points; 2.4 pp from private insurer overhead growth
- Growth driven mainly by Medicare/Medicaid managed-care plan overhead

**Relevance**: Strongest evidence that multipayer system is the primary driver of administrative costs. The $812B figure for 2017 would be ~$950B+ in 2024 dollars.

### 3. Billing & Insurance-Related Costs — Jiwani et al. (2014) ⭐

**Citation**: Jiwani A, Himmelstein D, Woolhandler S, Kahn JG. "Billing and insurance-related administrative costs in United States' health care: synthesis of micro-costing evidence." *BMC Health Services Research*. 2014;14:556.  
**DOI**: [10.1186/s12913-014-0556-7](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12913-014-0556-7)  
**PMID**: 25540104

**Key findings**:
- Total billing & insurance-related (BIR) costs in 2012: **$471B** ($330B-$597B range)
  - Physician practices: $70B ($54B-$76B)
  - Hospitals: $74B ($58B-$94B)
  - Other health services: $94B ($47B-$141B)
  - Private insurers: $198B ($154B-$233B)
  - Public insurers: $35B ($17B-$52B)
- "Added" BIR (above single-payer benchmark): **$375B** ($254B-$507B), or **80% of total BIR**
- A simplified financing system could save >$350B annually (~15% of spending)

**Relevance**: Micro-costing approach gives granular view of where admin costs sit. Shows 80% of BIR is "added" by multipayer complexity.

### 4. Hospital Administrative Costs — 8 Nations — Himmelstein et al. (2014)

**Citation**: Himmelstein DU, Jun M, Busse R, et al. "A comparison of hospital administrative costs in eight nations: US costs exceed all others by far." *Health Affairs*. 2014;33(9):1586-1594.  
**DOI**: [10.1377/hlthaff.2013.1327](https://doi.org/10.1377/hlthaff.2013.1327)  
**PMID**: 25201663

**Key findings**:
- US hospital admin costs: **25.3% of total hospital expenditures** (and increasing)
- Next highest: Netherlands 19.8%, England 15.5%
- Lowest: Scotland and Canada (single-payer, global budgets)
- Reducing to Scottish/Canadian levels would save **>$150 billion** (2011 dollars)
- Market-oriented payment systems correlate with higher admin costs

**Relevance**: Directly links payment system complexity to hospital overhead.

### 5. Private vs. Medicare Hospital Prices — RAND (2022) ⭐

**Citation**: Whaley CM, Briscombe B, Kerber R, O'Neill B, Kofner A. "Prices Paid to Hospitals by Private Health Plans: Findings from Round 4 of an Employer-Led Transparency Initiative." RAND Corporation, 2022. RR-A1144-1.  
**URL**: [https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1144-1.html](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA1144-1.html)

**Key findings**:
- In 2020, private insurers paid **224% of Medicare rates** for same services at same facilities
- Among common data contributors, prices averaged **252% of Medicare**
- State variation: Hawaii/Arkansas/Washington <175% vs. Florida/WV/SC ≥310%
- ASC prices averaged 162% of Medicare
- COVID-19 hospitalizations: 241% of Medicare
- **Hospital market power** explains more price variation than payer mix
- Very little variation explained by Medicare/Medicaid patient share

**Relevance**: Critical for Approach 3 (claims bottom-up). If Medicare pays X, private pays ~2.2-2.5X. This helps extrapolate from Medicare to total spending.

### 6. Universal Healthcare Pricing — Gaffney et al. (2021)

**Citation**: Gaffney A, Himmelstein DU, Woolhandler S, Kahn JG. "Pricing Universal Health Care: How Much Would The Use Of Medical Care Rise?" *Health Affairs*. 2021;40(1):105-112.  
**DOI**: [10.1377/hlthaff.2020.01715](https://doi.org/10.1377/hlthaff.2020.01715)  
**PMID**: 33400569

**Key findings**:
- Most M4A analyses project increased utilization with expanded coverage
- Actual utilization surges from past expansions have been modest
- Reviews evidence on how utilization changes when coverage expands

**Relevance**: Important for understanding what "true demand" for healthcare looks like vs. current constrained access.

## 📚 Books

### America's Bitter Pill (Steven Brill, 2015)

**Key themes** (from reviews, summaries, and Brill's original TIME article "Bitter Pill" 2013):

#### Hospital Chargemaster Pricing
- Hospitals maintain "chargemasters" — lists of prices for every service, supply, and procedure
- These prices bear no relation to cost, market rates, or any rational methodology
- A single Tylenol tablet can be billed at $1.50 (cost: $0.01)
- Chargemaster prices are the "sticker price" used as starting point for negotiations
- Uninsured patients are often billed full chargemaster rates
- Medicare pays ~1/3 to 1/4 of chargemaster prices and hospitals still profit
- The existence of chargemasters enables the entire pricing opacity

#### Pharmaceutical & Device Industry Lobbying
- PhRMA spent ~$200M/year on lobbying (more than any other industry)
- Medicare Part D explicitly prohibited from negotiating drug prices (until IRA 2022)
- Device manufacturers charge enormous markups (e.g., joint implants costing $350 to manufacture sold for $10,000+)
- "Most profitable lobbying investment in history" — preventing Medicare drug price negotiation

#### Insurance Complexity
- Insurance companies employ armies of claims processors, deniers, and appeals handlers
- Prior authorization creates enormous administrative burden on providers
- The billing/coding ecosystem employs millions of people who produce no healthcare
- ACA's MLR requirements forced some transparency but left fundamental structure intact

#### Data Sources Brill Used
- Hospital chargemaster documents (obtained through reporting)
- CMS Medicare payment data
- Hospital financial filings (Form 990 for nonprofits)
- IRS data on nonprofit hospital executive compensation
- Open Payments data (industry payments to physicians)
- Interviews with hospital administrators, patients, insurers

**Relevance**: Provides narrative evidence for the pricing failures identified by Shrank et al. The chargemaster system is a key mechanism for the $230-240B pricing failure waste.

## 🔬 Additional Studies to Cite

### Time-Driven Activity-Based Costing (TDABC)
- **Kaplan RS, Porter ME.** Pioneered TDABC application to healthcare at Harvard Business School
- **Hernandez A, Kaplan RS, Witkowski ML, Faison III CF, Porter ME.** "Navy Medicine Introduces Value-Based Health Care." *Health Affairs*. 2019;38(8):1393-1400. DOI: [10.1377/hlthaff.2019.00280](https://doi.org/10.1377/hlthaff.2019.00280). PMID: 31381402
- TDABC measures actual resources (time, supplies) consumed per patient per condition
- Reveals true cost of care delivery, typically far below billed amounts

### Low-Value Care Trends
- **Mafi JN et al.** "Trends in Low-Value Health Service Use and Spending in the US Medicare Fee-for-Service Program, 2014-2018." *JAMA Network Open*. 2021;4(2):e2037328. DOI: [10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2020.37328](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2020.37328). PMID: 33591365

## 🌍 International Comparisons

### Commonwealth Fund — US Health Care from a Global Perspective (2022)
**URL**: https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/issue-briefs/2023/jan/us-health-care-global-perspective-2022  
*(Note: Page blocked by Cloudflare; findings from known publications)*

**Key points from Commonwealth Fund comparative studies**:
- US spends roughly **twice** the OECD average per capita on healthcare
- US has **worse outcomes** on many measures (life expectancy, infant mortality, preventable deaths)
- US administrative costs are highest among peer nations
- US has fewer physicians and hospital beds per capita than many peers
- US prices (not utilization) are the primary driver of higher spending
- Famous conclusion: **"It's the prices, stupid"** (Anderson et al., *Health Affairs* 2003)

### Anderson et al. (2003) — Foundational
**Citation**: Anderson GF, Reinhardt UE, Hussey PS, Petrosyan V. "It's The Prices, Stupid: Why The United States Is So Different From Other Countries." *Health Affairs*. 2003;22(3):89-105.  
**DOI**: [10.1377/hlthaff.22.3.89](https://doi.org/10.1377/hlthaff.22.3.89)

**Key finding**: US healthcare spending differences vs. other nations are driven primarily by **higher prices**, not higher utilization. Americans don't use more healthcare — they pay more for it.
